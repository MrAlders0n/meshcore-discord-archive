The gist of what I gathered exploring whether I wanted to invest more in this strategy is as follows: Due to the packet structures, you'll take probably a 25% - 50% performance penalty of tunneling RNS over MeshCore. There's a chance of MeshCore falling back to flood routing in the event that smarter routing isn't available so you could end up spamming a larger portion of the network than intended. Finally, they (RNS and MeshCore) will be competing for resources whereas even operating them on the same frequency, you'll get better performance with separate networks and radio hardware - due to the 1% transmit time stipulation of the unlicensed ~900mhz bands.

Comms_Engineer 2025-11-19 3:33 p.m.
For the sake of discussion, a couple thoughts: 1) RNS's routing design seems to be inherently more capable of handling global-type networks than MeshCore. Because of this, being able to leverage existing transport such as MeshCore when alternatives aren't available is attractive. 2) Because of the amount of investment in MeshCore infrastructure already, I don't think it's reasonable to think that a buildout of RNS-specific LoRa nodes providing the same amount of coverage is in the cards. At a minimum, under current situations you're talking about every MeshCore repeater node adding a second LoRa module that's configured to run RNS-specific microcontroller firmware (doesn't currently exist), establishing a second standardized frequency, and adding a splitter/combiner to let both nodes use the same antenna. 3) I think the absolute ideal solution would be if MeshCore natively supported RNS traffic, with the current MeshCore implementation (public/private chats) acting as a sort of orderwire for the RNS network.
