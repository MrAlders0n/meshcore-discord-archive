868MHz (UK/EU/SA) and 915MHz (US) are the most common frequencies used on Meshcore and Meshtastic at present.
Delta1 2025-11-17 6:01 a.m.
Problem 433 MHz faces in South Afrca is that power outputs are limited to 10dBm which is 10mW (unless you have a valid HAM license and then you can only send unencrypted traffic) while in the 868MHz band you are allowed up to 27dBm which is 500mW (1% duty cycle). Even though theoretically the 433MHz band transmits further in obstructed terrain for the same power output the difference between 10mW and 500mW is so great that 868MHz exceeds 433Mhz range.
6:05 a.m.
433 MHz Band: This band is widely used globally and in South Africa for low-cost, short-range devices such as garage door openers, wireless alarms, remote controls, weather stations, and even signal jammers. Its popularity leads to higher congestion, especially in urban areas like Johannesburg or Cape Town, where imported consumer electronics flood the market. Sources indicate overcrowding from low-quality hardware and common applications, resulting in more ambient noise, harmonics, and low-frequency interference. Local reports from South Africa highlight the use of 433 MHz for illegal signal jammers in crimes like home invasions and cash-in-transit heists, which could introduce sporadic but significant interference. Measurements and comparisons (not SA-specific but applicable due to similar regulations) show this band experiences more disturbance from coexisting devices.
6:06 a.m.
868 MHz Band: Allocated more specifically for wireless sensor networks, IoT, and telemetry in regions following CEPT/ERC standards (which South Africa aligns with via ICASA's National Radio Frequency Plan), this band sees less everyday consumer traffic. It's less prone to interference from ubiquitous devices, making it "cleaner" for reliable long-range, low-power communications. While it can face some overlap with other ISM users, the overall occupancy is lower, with fewer reports of harmonics or noise compared to 433 MHz. In practice, 868 MHz is often recommended for applications needing stability over raw penetration in moderately interfered environments.

Delta1 2025-12-06 7:15 a.m.
Unlike client nodes on MT companion nodes on MC don't relay messages. MC relies on routers for that.
Delta1 2025-12-06 7:27 a.m.
Keeps mesh traffic lower. MC floods to find the best path between companion nodes and then sets that as the route. Only the routers on that best path relay messages which saves on airtime. If the path fails after a 3rd retry it floods to find a new best path. MC doesn't automatically transmit telemetry. This also saves airtime. MC has a hop limit of 64 if I am remembering correctly. It has less issues with network congestion for the above reasons. There are so many client nodes on MT in Cape Town (in particular) that the mesh is heavily congested and nodes even slightly further away from each other have unreliable connections...unless using MQTT but then MQTT defeats the object of having off grid comms.
