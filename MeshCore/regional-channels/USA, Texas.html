Uncle Lit 2025-10-28 2:42 p.m.
Hi Andrew! Welcome to meshcore! SF11 and BW250 might have a longer range in an isolated 2-node test. But that is just a setting that we borrowed from meshtastic when we didn't know better. the narrow bandwidth in the USA/Canada (Recommended) preset definitely works better with a couple repeaters in place. SF7 is a lot more responsive in message delivery, it feels almost like using iMessage, SMS, Signals, etc. With narrow bandwidth at 62.5, it fits in between all the water and power meters that are in use everywhere in north America (USA and Canada). The interference signals from these water and power meters are especially detrimental to high elevation repeaters because these repeaters will hear everything including all these interference signals within tens of miles, so narrow bandwidth ends up working better than alternate (really should be named deprecated). The biggest meshcore meshes around the world, Puget Sound + Salish Mesh (Vancouver, Victoria, Seattle, Tacoma, Portland), Boston, Austin, UK, EU, Australia, New Zealand, Czech, are all on narrow 62.5 bandwidth. Take a look at the puget sound mesh map here: https://analyzer.letsme.sh/map?lat=47.6&long=-122.3&zoom=8 another reason to use Recommended is because MeshCore can have up to 64 hops (vs meshtastic's 7-hop limit), there is a very good chance you can link up to your neighboring cities' mesh if you are on the same preset. By using the same Recommend preset, we can send and receive messages from Vancouver, BC, to Victoria, Seattle, Tacoma, Olympia, down to Portland and Eugene, OR. In a few more months, may be a year, we are confident we will link up to the Bay Area in California.

Uncle Lit 2025-10-28 3:15 p.m.
since 62.5 is much narrower than 250, we compensate it with SF7 to get the speed back and then some. that is why narrow is so fast. with higher SF number, it takes longer to transmit. another advantage of faster transmission is that you can get off the airwave much faster, giving other nodes opportunities to send packets out and further minimizing packet collisions. It makes for a faster, healthier mesh. LoRa is actually very resilient. we have had 150 miles transmissions from Victoria, BC to our main repeater on Cougar Mountain in Seattle. The key is having clear line of sight and high elavation.

garrisonsan 2026-01-20 4:19 p.m.
With region filtering/scopes becoming part of the main client app (https://buymeacoffee.com/ripplebiz/region-filtering) and hopefully with more mesh growth in the state, I'd like to suggest the following naming conventions of scopes for repeater owners to add to their repeaters as desired so that at least we have a standardized way of doing things: #texas - to pass on any traffic tagged for a statewide channel/mesh, if one ever develops #abcreg - a regional area scope using the name of the most recognized or generally popular city or colloquial term (e.g. #dfwreg for Dallas/Ft. Worth, #houreg for Houston, maybe #capreg - capitol region - for Austin/San Antonio, etc.). Smaller cities can still take advantage of this, too. I'd suggest using the last three characters of the ICAO code for the nearest regional airport (e.g. #lbbreg for Lubbock, #mafreg for Midland/Odessa, #amareg for amarillo, #spsreg for Wichita Falls, etc.) #hometownco - county level scope where needed #hometownco-east - geographical subdivision of a county level scope #cityname - city level scope #cityname-north - geographical subdivision of a city level scope As the linked article outlines, so long as repeater owners don't remove the "*" scope (present by default) from their repeaters, all flood packets received will still be forwarded, so there's no downside to planning ahead and setting up scopes early at the local/regional level. Then if/when traffic management becomes an issue, removing the wildcard scope is an easy change to make.

NoahK 2026-03-20 5:03 p.m.
Lora uses frequency chirps for each bit, and at different spreading factors with the same bandwidth, these chirps happen at different (multiples of 2) rates. A receiver configured to listen in on one spreading factor is effectively tuned out of the other spreading factor (cross-correlation <10%), even if they share the same frequency center. https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/stamp/stamp.jsp?tp=&arnumber=10609524, Figure 12. There is also research concluding that the orthogonality is not perfect, but if the lora preamble is successfully rx'd, then the rest of the packet will be too.
